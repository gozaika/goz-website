====
Yes, with one important adjustment: **schema first is right, but do not do schema → all middleware → all frontend as three giant phases.** Your own spec already points in the right direction: build the data model before UI, and build the API before the client. But in practice, once the foundational schema is in place, you should implement in **vertical slices**: auth/profile slice, restaurant onboarding slice, bag template/drop slice, claim/payment slice, pickup verification slice, then notifications, analytics, and admin. That reduces drift, catches schema mistakes earlier, and avoids spending weeks building middleware for features whose UX and real-world edge cases are still untested. 

A second correction: do **not** try to make the first schema “comprehensive” in the sense of modeling every possible future feature equally deeply on day one. The business doc and tech spec clearly show a hard P0 core around users, restaurants, bag templates, drops, orders, payments, subscriptions, referrals, reviews, auditability, pickup verification, DPDP compliance, and merchant financials. Analytics, demand forecasting, POS sync, corporate ordering, Shopify cross-sell, and some gamification can sit on top of that core later as either extension tables or derived models. The documents emphasize sell-through, pickup completion, complaints/refunds, retention, and merchant ROI as the operational core, not speculative breadth.

Because you are moving from AWS-managed primitives to **Vercel + Supabase**, your schema should be designed around **Supabase Postgres as the system of record**, **Supabase Auth identities mapped to your own role/profile tables**, **Supabase Storage** for media/documents, **RLS-ready tenancy boundaries**, **database-enforced state transitions where possible**, and **an outbox/events pattern** for notifications, webhooks, and async work. The original spec explicitly valued PostgreSQL, Prisma, row-level security, audit logging, atomic inventory behavior, and strong separation of audiences/roles; those principles still hold even though Cognito/RDS/S3/Redis are being replaced.

The biggest launch-risk if Cursor generates the schema naïvely is that it will mirror the sample tables from Section D too literally and miss several business-critical capabilities that are only implied elsewhere in the documents: merchant staff/accounts, role scoping, recurring drop scheduling, inventory mutation history, payment/refund ledgers, settlements and invoices, complaint and incident handling, consent granularity, DPDP erasure workflow, restaurant follow/save behavior, notification preferences, offline pickup verification support, and configuration/feature flags. Those are all required by the business and product docs even if they are not fully represented in the short sample schema.

So my recommendation is:

1. Freeze the **domain model and invariants** first.
2. Generate the schema and SQL migration set.
3. Immediately build **one vertical slice** end to end: auth/profile + restaurant + bag template + drop create/publish.
4. Then do claim/payment/pickup, because that is where your hardest correctness problems live: inventory race conditions, payment idempotency, order state transitions, and refund logic.

### Non-negotiable design principles

Design the schema according to these principles:

* PostgreSQL is the **single source of truth**.
* Schema must work well with **Supabase Auth**.
* Use **UUID primary keys** everywhere except append-only audit/event tables where BIGSERIAL is acceptable.
* Prefer normalized relational design for transactional data.
* Use JSONB only where flexibility is truly justified.
* Money must be stored as **integer paise** or integer rupees consistently. Pick one strategy and apply it consistently. Explain your choice.
* Use **timestamptz** everywhere for timestamps.
* Every mutable business table needs `created_at` and `updated_at`.
* Soft delete only where legally or operationally needed. Otherwise use explicit archival/status fields.
* Model **state machines explicitly**; do not rely on free-text statuses.
* Design for **RLS / multi-tenant isolation** from day one.
* Design for **DPDP compliance**, explicit consent, purpose limitation, retention, and erasure workflows.
* Design for **food safety and merchant accountability**.
* Design for **analytics derivation**, but do not over-denormalize core transactional tables.
* Design for **idempotent payment/webhook handling**.
* Design for **auditability** of critical actions.
* Design for **offline-tolerant restaurant staff pickup verification**.
* Design for **launch practicality**: not every Phase 2/3 feature needs full depth if a lighter representation is cleaner.
